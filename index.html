Why This Exists

Good companies sometimes lose their way.

Management doesn't always have bad intentions. But pressure, growth, and complexity can cause leaders to lose sight of what matters most — the people doing the work. You shouldn't have to suffer through that. You have rights, and you deserve to know them.

Section 7 — NLRA

What the law says you can do.

Section 7 of the National Labor Relations Act is one of the most powerful — and least understood — worker protection laws in the United States. It applies to most private-sector employees, whether you're in a union or not.

At its core, Section 7 protects your right to take collective action with your coworkers to improve your working conditions. You do not need to be in a union for these rights to apply. They apply right now, today, at your current job.

The Law — Plain English
"Employees have the right to organize, form or join a union, bargain collectively, and engage in concerted activities for mutual aid or protection."
✓
Discuss Pay With Coworkers
You have the right to discuss wages, hours, and conditions with coworkers. Your employer cannot legally prohibit this.
✓
Organize With Coworkers
You can work with coworkers to address shared concerns — formally or informally — even without forming a union.
✓
Sign or Circulate Petitions
Collectively petitioning your employer about workplace conditions is protected activity under federal law.
✓
Refuse Unsafe Work
If two or more employees refuse genuinely unsafe work together, that concerted action is protected under Section 7.
✓
Join or Form a Union
You have the absolute right to join an existing union or organize a new one. Your employer cannot legally prevent this.
✓
Choose Not to Join
Section 7 also protects your right to decline union membership. No one can force you to join.
Section 8 — NLRA

What your employer cannot do.

Section 8 defines Unfair Labor Practices — specific actions that are illegal for employers. If any of these have happened to you, you may have a legal claim.

Illegal
Threaten or Retaliate
Your employer cannot threaten to fire, demote, or punish you for engaging in protected activity — including talking to coworkers about working conditions.
Illegal
Spy on Organizing Activity
Employers cannot surveil or monitor employees engaged in organizing — including through social media monitoring or informants.
Illegal
Interfere With Your Right to Organize
Any action that coerces or interferes with employees exercising Section 7 rights is an Unfair Labor Practice reportable to the NLRB.
Illegal
Prohibit Pay Discussions
Any policy — written or verbal — prohibiting employees from discussing wages with each other is illegal under the NLRA.
Illegal
Create a Company-Controlled Union
Employers cannot create, control, or fund a company union or employee committee that substitutes for genuine independent representation.
Illegal
Discriminate Based on Union Activity
Hiring, firing, promotion, and scheduling decisions cannot legally be based on an employee's union membership or organizing activity.
Real Scenarios — What the Law Covers
Situation
Your manager says you'll be fired if you keep talking to coworkers about pay.
What the Law Says
This is illegal. Discussing wages is explicitly protected under Section 7. A termination threat for this is an Unfair Labor Practice you can report to the NLRB.
Situation
You and two coworkers signed a letter to HR about unsafe conditions. A week later, all three received negative performance reviews.
What the Law Says
This may be illegal retaliation. Collective action on safety is protected. The timing of negative reviews following protected activity is a pattern the NLRB takes seriously.
An Honest Look

What unions can and can't do for you.

Unionization is a legal right, and sometimes it's the right call. But it's a significant, long-term decision — and you deserve a clear picture of both sides before you decide anything.

✊
What Unions Can Do
  • Give employees a collective voice in negotiations over wages, hours, and conditions
  • Provide legal representation if you face discipline or termination
  • Create formal grievance processes that must be followed
  • The threat of unionization alone often motivates employers to improve conditions — even without an election
  • Establish written contracts that define and protect employment terms
⚖️
What Unions Cannot Guarantee
  • Unions cannot guarantee wage increases — contracts result from negotiation, not automatic raises
  • The process is slow — organizing, elections, and contract negotiations can take years
  • Dues are required — typically 1–2% of wages — regardless of outcomes
  • Adversarial dynamics can create a permanently tense workplace for everyone
  • Individual issues may become secondary to the union's broader agenda
"Often, the decision to organize is most powerful before it's acted on. The moment an employer understands their workforce is serious, many of the issues that drove people to that point become solvable."
— WorkplaceVoice Advisory Team
